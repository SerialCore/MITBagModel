\begin{abstract}
In this work, we estimate the masses and magnetic moments for ground-state mesons, baryons, singly heavy tetraquarks with strange number $S=1$ and doubly heavy tetraquarks, in an unified description of MIT bag model and CMI model.
For light hadrons, we propose a new running behavior of strong coupling constant which is sufficient to reduce errors for the whole mass spectra significantly.
For heavy sectors, we use masses of heavy mesons with $J^{P}=1^{-}$ to determine the effective masses of charm and bottom quarks, as well as binding energies which are assumed to exist among them and strange quarks.
The mass and magnetic moments of $\Xi_{cc}^{\ast}$ are predicted to be $M\left(\Xi_{cc}^{\ast}\right)=3.714\,{\rm GeV}$, $\mu\left(\Xi_{cc}^{\ast ++}\right)=0.94\mu_{p}$ and $\mu\left(\Xi_{cc}^{\ast +}\right)=0.06\mu_{p}$.
The mass of isosinglet $ud\bar{s}\bar{c}$ with $J^{P}=0^{+}$ are predicted to be $M\left(ud\bar{s}\bar{c}\right)=2.934\,{\rm GeV}$.
Our results imply that MIT bag model can be universal for all the ground-state hadrons.

\maketitle\section{Introduction}
Physical pictures

In the past few decades, many applications of MIT bag model on hadron spectrum or magnetic moment have been published.
For light hadrons, multiquark baryons with five and seven $n$ (denotes $u$, $d$) quarks were studied in Ref.\cite{Strottman:1979qu}.
A new concept of refinement of parameters was proposed to calculate light hadrons and glueballs \cite{Carlson:1982er}.
For heavy hadrons, Refs.\cite{Haxton:1980mc,Aerts:1980rf} derived Coulomb-like potentials between heavy quarks in a bag.
Refs.\cite{Izatt:1981pt,Fleck:1989mb,He:2004px} estimated the mass spectra of doubly heavy baryons, and Ref.\cite{Bernotas:2012nz} evaluated their magnetic moments in details.
These works endow us with knowledge of physical pictures and methods for MIT bag model. However, they only discuss specific hadron systems with corresponding parameters.
We can fill the vacancy of rest hadrons in this work with a universal set of parameters.

In 2017, LHCb discovered the doubly charmed baryon $\Xi_{cc}^{++}$ with $J^{P}={1/2}^{+}$ and mass $3621.40\pm 0.78\,{\rm MeV}$ \cite{Aaij:2017ueg}.
After the first observation of $\Xi_{cc}^{+}$ in 2002 \cite{Moinester:2002uw}, it attracted many theorists in predicting mass spectra and magnetic moments.
Several works with varied models and theories studied $\Xi_{cc}$ systems, further doubly heavy baryons \cite{Fleck:1989mb,Ebert:2004ck,Bernotas:2012nz,He:2004px,Karliner:2014gca,Liu:2018euh}.
Some of them in MIT bag model have underestimated the mass spectra. In this work, we will restudy this model for ground-state hadrons to refine the predictions of $\Xi_{cc}$.

Recently, LHCb has reported the first exotic state $X_{0}(2900)$ with open heavy flavor and mass $2866\pm 7\,{\rm MeV}$ \cite{LHCb:2020kd}.
M.~Karliner interprets it as an isosinglet tetraquark $cs\bar{u}\bar{d}$ in Ref.~\cite{Karliner:2020vsi}, by string-junction picture of quark model, giving a good prediction of mass spectrum. 
We will also calculate $nn\bar{s}\bar{c}$ and $nn\bar{s}\bar{b}$ systems via MIT bag model, which holds controllable errors for mesons and baryons.
Nevertheless, our model using variational method and chromomagnetic mixing will have a larger result of $nn\bar{s}\bar{c}$ comparing with experimental data and that of quark model, thus make the contrary conclusion.

In this work, we start with mass spectra of light hadrons keeping most free parameters in MIT bag model, and introduce a new running behavior of $\alpha_{s}$.
Differing from previous works of bag model, this running constant varies from $0.4$ to $0.6$ when bag radius $R=3\sim 6\,{\rm {GeV}^{-1}}$, providing suppressed values and significant variation.
The new running $\alpha_{s}$ can remarkably reduce the errors for heavy mesons, and induce a stable model error $40\,{\rm MeV}$ for heavy baryons, from which we further proceed to tetraquarks.
We then use $D^{\ast}$ and $B^{\ast}$ to determine the masses of $c$ and $b$ quarks, and $D_{s}^{\ast}$, $B_{s}^{\ast}$, $J/\psi$, $\Upsilon$, $B_{c}^{\ast}$ to fit short-disctance binding energies, as universal parameters for the whole hadron spectrum.
Once the confirmed states are calculated with good estimations, we predict masses and magnetic moments of doubly and triply heavy baryons with chromomagnetic mixing considered.
Finally, we study singly heavy tetraquarks $nn\bar{s}\bar{c}$, $nn\bar{s}\bar{b}$ and doubly heavy sectors.

The advantages of our method are to offer the readers clear physical pictures about hadron mass, and to calculate almost all the ground-state spectra in a row with a unified description of MIT bag model.
During this procedure, the running $\alpha_{s}$ and binding energies that can reduce errors significantly will help achieve this goal.

This paper will be organized as follows. In Sec.~\ref{Method}, we introduce some basic formulae of MIT bag model and Chromomagnetic Interaction (CMI) model. 
In Sec.~\ref{Parameters}, we perform a series of numerical calculations to find out the optimal set of parameters for confirmed states. 
This section presents the mass spectra, magnetic moments and charge radii for non-mixing light mesons, baryons and heavy sectors. 
In Sec.~\ref{Hadrons}, we predict and discuss the masses of mixing baryons and tetraquarks. 
Finally, this paper ends with summaries and conclusions in Sec.~\ref{Discussions}.


\section{Method for MIT bag model and CMI}\label{Method}
\subsection{Mass Formula}
In MIT bag model \cite{Johnson:1975zp,DeGrand:1975cf}, the mass formula of hadron is
\begin{equation}\label{MBm}
    M\left(R\right)=\sum_{i=n,s,c,b} n_{i}\omega_{i}+\frac{4}{3}\pi R^{3}B-\frac{Z_{0}}{R}+\Delta M
\end{equation}
\begin{equation}\label{freq}
    \omega_{i}=\left(m_{i}^{2}+\frac{x_{i}^{2}}{R^2}\right)^{1/2}
\end{equation}
where $n_{i}$ is number of quark or antiquark for $n$, $s$, $c$, $b$ flavor, $m_{i}$ is mass, $x_{i}=x_{i}(mR)$ is dimensionless dynamical parameters, 
$R$ is bag radius, $B$ is bag constant and $Z_{0}$ is zero-point-energy (ZPE) constant. 
$\Delta M$ is interaction energy which we address in this work.
We choose $m_{i}$, $B$, $Z_{0}$ as free parameters and treat $x_{i}$, $R$ as intermediate parameters satisfying a transcendental equation
\begin{equation}\label{transc}
    \tan x_{i}=\frac{x_{i}}{1-m_{i}R-\left(m_{i}^{2}R^{2}+x_{i}^{2}\right)^{1/2}}
\end{equation}

The first part of hadron mass in Eq.~(\ref{MBm}) is sum of relativistical energy of quarks contained in the bag.
With the interaction energy $\Delta M$ including Coulomb-like and spin-dependent potentials, it contributes to quark model.
But MIT bag model offers more physical pictures about QCD, 
that the second part is volume energy in which constant $B$ standing for color confinement indicates difference of energy density between perturbation and non-perturbation QCD vacuum.
And the third part is ZPE produced by Casimir effect in a sphere bag.

The mass formula (\ref{MBm}) is a function of bag radius $R$, and most terms in it are also $R$-dependent.
Because it’s believed that a sphere bag surface can be balanced by Dirac pressure inside and vacuum condensation outside. 
For the stability, bag energy will locate at minimum with respect to bag radius $R$. 
That means we need only a few free parameters to calculate all the lowest-lying hadronic states, from mesons to tetraquarks, by variational method.
And we would rather consider recoil of heavy quark, than taking replacement of the first term in Eq.~(\ref{MBm}) by fixed constituent mass.

The interaction energy $\Delta M$ in Eq.~(\ref{MBm}) contains Coulomb-like and spin-dependent potentials.
In Refs.~\cite{Karliner:2014gca}, M.~Karliner has introduced binding effect between two relatively heavy quarks, in studying quark model.
This effect is thought of as short-disctance chromoelectric interactions between c and s, c and c, b and s, b and b, b and c quarks respectively, 
which form strong bindings namely binding energy. In this work, we will have five bindings $B_{cs}$, $B_{cc}$, $B_{bs}$, $B_{bb}$, $B_{bc}$ for color $\boldsymbol{\bar{3}}$, 
and scale them by color factors to other color configurations.

The second part of $\Delta M$ is chromomagnetic interaction
\begin{equation}\label{CMI}
    H_{CMI}=-\sum_{i<j} \left(\boldsymbol{\lambda_{i}}\cdot \boldsymbol{\lambda_{j}}\right) \left(\boldsymbol{\sigma_{i}}\cdot \boldsymbol{\sigma_{j}}\right)C_{ij}
\end{equation}
where $i$ and $j$ denote quarks or antiquarks, $\boldsymbol{\lambda}$ and $\boldsymbol{\sigma}$ are Gell-Mann and Pauli matrices respectively. 
$C_{ij}$ is dynamical parameter which can be derived by MIT bag model as
\begin{equation}\label{Cij}
    C_{ij}=3\frac{\alpha_{s}\left(R\right)}{R^{3}}\bar{\mu}_{i}\bar{\mu}_{j}I_{ij}
\end{equation}
\begin{equation}\label{muBari}
   \bar{\mu}_{i}=\frac{R}{6} \frac{4\alpha_{i}+2\lambda_{i}-3}{2\alpha_{i}\left(\alpha_{i}-1\right)+\lambda_{i}}
\end{equation}
\begin{equation}\label{Iij}
    I_{ij}=1+2\int_{0}^{R} \frac{{\rm d}r}{r^{4}} \bar{\mu}_{i}\bar{\mu}_{j}=
    1+\text{rational term about } x_{i} \text{, } x_{j}
\end{equation}
where $\alpha_{i}\equiv\omega_{i}R$, $\lambda_{i}\equiv m_{i}R$, $\alpha_{s}\left(R\right)$ is running strong coupling constant, $\bar{\mu}_{i}$ is reduced magnetic moment without quark charge,
and $I_{ij}$ denoting improper integral has a complicated rational expression one can find in Ref.~\cite{DeGrand:1975cf}. 
In Sec.~\ref{Parameters}, $\alpha_{s}\left(R\right)$ will be discussed and chose for fitting spectrum well.


\subsection{Chromomagnetic Structure}
The chromomagnetic structure for a hadronic state is required \cite{Luo:2017eub}, if one needs to determine the expression of Eq.~(\ref{CMI}) after identifying this state with certian $J^{P}$ quantum number.
In this section, we will mainly introduce the flavor-spin-color wave functions for meson, baryon and tetraquark systems.

Mesons have color singlet and two spin states
\begin{equation}\label{colorM}
    \phi^{M}=\left| q_{1}\bar{q}_{2} \right\rangle
\end{equation}
\begin{equation}\label{spinM}
    \chi_{1}^{M}={\left| q_{1}\bar{q}_{2} \right\rangle}_{1} \quad \chi_{2}^{M}={\left| q_{1}\bar{q}_{2} \right\rangle}_{0}
\end{equation}
where subscript outside the bracket denotes quantum number $J$. The corresponding combinations give
\begin{equation}\label{colorspinM}
    \phi^{M}\chi_{1}^{M}={\left| q_{1}\bar{q}_{2} \right\rangle}_{1} \quad \phi^{M}\chi_{2}^{M}={\left| q_{1}\bar{q}_{2} \right\rangle}_{0}
\end{equation}

Baryons have color $\boldsymbol{\bar{3}}$ and three spin states
\begin{equation}\label{colorB}
    \phi^{B}=\left| {\left(q_{1}q_{2}\right)}^{\bar{3}} q_{3} \right\rangle
\end{equation}
\begin{equation}\label{spinB}
    \begin{aligned}
        \chi_{1}^{B}={\left| {\left(q_{1}q_{2}\right)}_{1} q_{3} \right\rangle}_{3/2} \\ 
        \chi_{2}^{B}={\left| {\left(q_{1}q_{2}\right)}_{1} q_{3} \right\rangle}_{1/2} \\
        \chi_{3}^{B}={\left| {\left(q_{1}q_{2}\right)}_{0} q_{3} \right\rangle}_{1/2}
    \end{aligned}
\end{equation}
where $\left(q_{1}q_{2}\right)$ is diquark with spin number 1 or 0.

We now move on to the flavor symmetry.
If the flavor wave function of $\left(q_{1}q_{2}\right)$ is symmetric, when isospin $I=1$ or flavors are identical, we choose a symbol $\delta_{12}^{S}=1$. 
Otherwise, if the flavor is asymmetric when $I=0$, there will be $\delta_{12}^{A}=1$. 
And if $q_{1}$ and $q_{2}$ carry different flavors beyond SU(2) isospin symmetry, we believe the wave function can be either symmetric or asymmetric namely $\delta_{12}^{S}=\delta_{12}^{A}=1$. 
Consequently, we obtain three flavor-spin-color wave functions according to Pauli principle writing in
\begin{equation}\label{colorspinB}
    \begin{aligned}
        \phi^{B}\chi_{1}^{B}={\left| {\left(q_{1}q_{2}\right)}_{1}^{\bar{3}} q_{3} \right\rangle}_{3/2} \delta_{12}^{S} \\
        \phi^{B}\chi_{2}^{B}={\left| {\left(q_{1}q_{2}\right)}_{1}^{\bar{3}} q_{3} \right\rangle}_{1/2} \delta_{12}^{S} \\
        \phi^{B}\chi_{3}^{B}={\left| {\left(q_{1}q_{2}\right)}_{0}^{\bar{3}} q_{3} \right\rangle}_{1/2} \delta_{12}^{A} 
    \end{aligned}
\end{equation}

In this work, we also study chromomagnetic mixing. 
For example, $\Xi_{bc}$ and $\Xi_{bc}^{\prime}$ with flavor structure $(bc)s$ choosing $\delta_{12}^{S}=\delta_{12}^{A}=1$ can't distinguish $\phi^{B}\chi_{2}^{B}$ and $\phi^{B}\chi_{3}^{B}$ states by $J^{P}$ quantum number, 
thus the physical state must be mixing of $\phi^{B}\chi_{2}^{B}$ and $\phi^{B}\chi_{3}^{B}$.
We will make full discussion about states mixing in Sec.~\ref{Parameters}.

Finally, tetraquarks have color $\boldsymbol{6}\otimes\boldsymbol{\bar{6}}$ and $\boldsymbol{\bar{3}}\otimes\boldsymbol{3}$ and six spin states
\begin{equation}\label{colorT}
    \phi_{1}^{T}=\left| {\left(q_{1}q_{2}\right)}^{6} {\left(\bar{q}_{3}\bar{q}_{4}\right)}^{\bar{6}} \right\rangle \quad
    \phi_{2}^{T}=\left| {\left(q_{1}q_{2}\right)}^{\bar{3}} {\left(\bar{q}_{3}\bar{q}_{4}\right)}^{3} \right\rangle
\end{equation}
\begin{equation}\label{spinT}
    \begin{aligned}
        \chi_{1}^{T}={\left| {\left(q_{1}q_{2}\right)}_{1} {\left(\bar{q}_{3}\bar{q}_{4}\right)}_{1} \right\rangle}_{2} \quad
        \chi_{2}^{T}={\left| {\left(q_{1}q_{2}\right)}_{1} {\left(\bar{q}_{3}\bar{q}_{4}\right)}_{1} \right\rangle}_{1} \\
        \chi_{3}^{T}={\left| {\left(q_{1}q_{2}\right)}_{1} {\left(\bar{q}_{3}\bar{q}_{4}\right)}_{1} \right\rangle}_{0} \quad
        \chi_{4}^{T}={\left| {\left(q_{1}q_{2}\right)}_{1} {\left(\bar{q}_{3}\bar{q}_{4}\right)}_{0} \right\rangle}_{1} \\
        \chi_{5}^{T}={\left| {\left(q_{1}q_{2}\right)}_{0} {\left(\bar{q}_{3}\bar{q}_{4}\right)}_{1} \right\rangle}_{1} \quad
        \chi_{6}^{T}={\left| {\left(q_{1}q_{2}\right)}_{0} {\left(\bar{q}_{3}\bar{q}_{4}\right)}_{0} \right\rangle}_{0}
    \end{aligned}
\end{equation}
with twelve wave functions
\begin{equation}\label{colorspinT}
    \begin{aligned}
        \phi_{1}^{T}\chi_{1}^{T}={\left| {\left(q_{1}q_{2}\right)}_{1}^{6} {\left(\bar{q}_{3}\bar{q}_{4}\right)}_{1}^{\bar{6}} \right\rangle}_{2} \delta_{12}^{A}\delta_{34}^{A} \\
        \phi_{2}^{T}\chi_{1}^{T}={\left| {\left(q_{1}q_{2}\right)}_{1}^{\bar{3}} {\left(\bar{q}_{3}\bar{q}_{4}\right)}_{1}^{3} \right\rangle}_{2} \delta_{12}^{S}\delta_{34}^{S} \\
        \phi_{1}^{T}\chi_{2}^{T}={\left| {\left(q_{1}q_{2}\right)}_{1}^{6} {\left(\bar{q}_{3}\bar{q}_{4}\right)}_{1}^{\bar{6}} \right\rangle}_{1} \delta_{12}^{A}\delta_{34}^{A} \\
        \phi_{2}^{T}\chi_{2}^{T}={\left| {\left(q_{1}q_{2}\right)}_{1}^{\bar{3}} {\left(\bar{q}_{3}\bar{q}_{4}\right)}_{1}^{3} \right\rangle}_{1} \delta_{12}^{S}\delta_{34}^{S} \\
        \phi_{1}^{T}\chi_{3}^{T}={\left| {\left(q_{1}q_{2}\right)}_{1}^{6} {\left(\bar{q}_{3}\bar{q}_{4}\right)}_{1}^{\bar{6}} \right\rangle}_{0} \delta_{12}^{A}\delta_{34}^{A} \\
        \phi_{2}^{T}\chi_{3}^{T}={\left| {\left(q_{1}q_{2}\right)}_{1}^{\bar{3}} {\left(\bar{q}_{3}\bar{q}_{4}\right)}_{1}^{3} \right\rangle}_{0} \delta_{12}^{S}\delta_{34}^{S} \\
        \phi_{1}^{T}\chi_{4}^{T}={\left| {\left(q_{1}q_{2}\right)}_{1}^{6} {\left(\bar{q}_{3}\bar{q}_{4}\right)}_{0}^{\bar{6}} \right\rangle}_{1} \delta_{12}^{A}\delta_{34}^{S} \\
        \phi_{2}^{T}\chi_{4}^{T}={\left| {\left(q_{1}q_{2}\right)}_{1}^{\bar{3}} {\left(\bar{q}_{3}\bar{q}_{4}\right)}_{0}^{3} \right\rangle}_{1} \delta_{12}^{S}\delta_{34}^{A} \\
        \phi_{1}^{T}\chi_{5}^{T}={\left| {\left(q_{1}q_{2}\right)}_{0}^{6} {\left(\bar{q}_{3}\bar{q}_{4}\right)}_{1}^{\bar{6}} \right\rangle}_{1} \delta_{12}^{S}\delta_{34}^{A} \\
        \phi_{2}^{T}\chi_{5}^{T}={\left| {\left(q_{1}q_{2}\right)}_{0}^{\bar{3}} {\left(\bar{q}_{3}\bar{q}_{4}\right)}_{1}^{3} \right\rangle}_{1} \delta_{12}^{A}\delta_{34}^{S} \\
        \phi_{1}^{T}\chi_{6}^{T}={\left| {\left(q_{1}q_{2}\right)}_{0}^{6} {\left(\bar{q}_{3}\bar{q}_{4}\right)}_{0}^{\bar{6}} \right\rangle}_{0} \delta_{12}^{S}\delta_{34}^{S} \\
        \phi_{2}^{T}\chi_{6}^{T}={\left| {\left(q_{1}q_{2}\right)}_{0}^{\bar{3}} {\left(\bar{q}_{3}\bar{q}_{4}\right)}_{0}^{3} \right\rangle}_{0} \delta_{12}^{A}\delta_{34}^{A}
    \end{aligned}
\end{equation}

Appendix A and B present all the precise color, spin wave functions. Therefore, the color and spin factors in Eq.~(\ref{CMI}) can be calculated by using
\begin{equation}\label{colorfc}
    {\left\langle \boldsymbol{\lambda_{i}}\cdot \boldsymbol{\lambda_{j}} \right\rangle}_{nm}=
    \sum_{\alpha=1}^{8} Tr\left(c_{in}^{\dagger}\lambda^{\alpha}c_{im}\right) Tr\left(c_{jn}^{\dagger}\lambda^{\alpha}c_{jm}\right)
\end{equation}
\begin{equation}\label{spinfc}
    {\left\langle \boldsymbol{\sigma_{i}}\cdot \boldsymbol{\sigma_{j}} \right\rangle}_{xy}=
    \sum_{\alpha=1}^{3} Tr\left(\chi_{ix}^{\dagger}\sigma^{\alpha}\chi_{iy}\right) Tr\left(\chi_{jx}^{\dagger}\sigma^{\alpha}\chi_{jy}\right)
\end{equation}
where $n$, $m$ and $x$, $y$ indicate the specific color and spin states respectively, $i$, $j$ are indexes of quarks or antiquarks and $c$, $\chi$ are basis vectors for color and spin space.
Several non-mixing hadronic states with CMI are listed in Table \ref{tab:CMI}.
We are now able to construct the whole mass formula (\ref{MBm}) and evaluate mass spectra and dynamical parameters $x_{i}$, $R$.

\subsection{Hadronic Properties}
After obtaining the parameters $x_{i}$, $R$ for a hadronic state, we subsequently calculate charge radius and magnetic moment,
which depend on dynamical information of quarks.
We first use the following mothed to evaluate contribution of quarks on charge radius \cite{DeGrand:1975cf}
\begin{equation}\label{rEi}
    \begin{aligned}
        {\left\langle r_{E}^{2} \right\rangle}_{i}&=
        Q_{i}R^{2} \frac{\alpha_{i} \left[2x_{i}^{2}\left(\alpha_{i}-1\right)+4\alpha_{i}+2\lambda_{i}-3\right]}
        {3x_{i}^{2} \left[2\alpha_{i}\left(\alpha_{i}-1\right)+\lambda_{i}\right]} \\ 
        &-Q_{i}R^{2} \frac{\lambda_{i} \left[4\alpha_{i}+2\lambda_{i}-2x_{i}^{2}-3\right]}
        {2x_{i}^{2} \left[2\alpha_{i}\left(\alpha_{i}-1\right)+\lambda_{i}\right]}
    \end{aligned}
\end{equation}
where $Q_{i}$ is charge of quark or antiquark. 
Since the spin-color wave functions cannot apply to charge radius, one can directly sum up Eq.~(\ref{rEi}) by
\begin{equation}\label{rEsum}
    r_{E}={\left| \sum\nolimits_{i} {\left\langle r_{E}^{2} \right\rangle}_{i} \right|}^{1/2}
\end{equation}
to give out the charge radius of hadronic state \cite{Chodos:1974pn}.
Eq.~(\ref{rEsum}) is also correct for mixing systems which have the same quark constituent.

We then use the following equation to calculate magnetic moment \cite{DeGrand:1975cf,Wang:2016dzu}
\begin{equation}\label{mui}
    \mu_{i}=Q_{i}\bar{\mu}_{i}=Q_{i}\frac{R}{6} \frac{4\alpha_{i}+2\lambda_{i}-3}{2\alpha_{i}\left(\alpha_{i}-1\right)+\lambda_{i}}
\end{equation}
\begin{equation}\label{musum}
    \mu=\left\langle \psi_{spin} \left| \sum\nolimits_{i}g_{i}\mu_{i}S_{iz} \right| \psi_{spin} \right\rangle
\end{equation}
where $S_{iz}$ is the third component of spin for individual quark or antiquark, and $g_{i}=2$ is a Lander factor. 
Moreover, if mixing states are took into account whose total spin wave function reads
\begin{equation}
    \left| \psi_{spin} \right\rangle=C_{1}\chi_{1}+C_{2}\chi_{2}
\end{equation}
one can express magnetic moment as
\begin{equation}\label{mixingmu}
    \mu=C_{1}^{2}\mu\left(\chi_{1}\right)+C_{2}^{2}\mu\left(\chi_{2}\right)+2C_{1}C_{2}\mu^{\rm tr}\left(\chi_{1}, \chi_{2}\right)
\end{equation}
where $\mu^{\rm tr}$ is transition moment studied in Ref.~\cite{Bernotas:2012nz} and $C_{1}$, $C_{2}$ form eigenvector for mixing states.
According to spin wave functions in Appendix B, we derive magnetic moments for all the spin states and mixing systems involved in this work, and list them in Table \ref{tab:musum}.

We notice that, the transition moments in mixing systems are not symmetric by swaping $q_{1}$, $q_{2}$ or $q_{3}$, $q_{4}$ in flavor wave functions respectively.
While isospin shall not be considered in constructing mass formula, it accually distinguishs magnetic moments, between $\left(ud\right)s$, $\left(du\right)s$ states for example,
by the isospin wave function
\begin{equation}
    {\left| 1,0\right\rangle}_{I}=\frac{1}{\sqrt{2}} \left(ud+du\right) \quad
    {\left| 0,0\right\rangle}_{I}=\frac{1}{\sqrt{2}} \left(ud-du\right)
\end{equation}
One must take average of these two states to evaluate magnetic moment physically, but consequently the transition moments vanish.
So there is no transition moment between two mixing state, and we will keep only the terms with $C_{i}^{2}$ in Eq.~(\ref{mixingmu}).

At the end of this section, we further mention that, for a state with precise flavor-spin-color structure and certian CMI, it has a unique set of $x_{i}$, $R$ comparing with others, even those states carring the same $J^{P}$ quantum number.
It restricts us to determine mass formula, for mixinig systems indicating analytical eigenvalues of CMI matrix, before performing variation to it.
One may keep this in mind when calculating mass spectra and hadronic properties of mixing states especially tetraquarks.


\section{Determination of Parameters}\label{Parameters}
MIT bag model only discusses a few free parameters such as quark mass $m_{i}$, bag constant $B$, coefficient $Z_{0}$ of ZPE and strong coupling constant $\alpha_{s}$.
We quote the original work \cite{Johnson:1975zp}, where the parameters are determined so that light hadrons $N$, $\Delta$, $\omega$ and $\Omega$ fit in spectrum, that suggests 
\begin{equation}\label{originalparas}
    \begin{Bmatrix}
        m_{n}=0\,{\rm GeV} & m_{s}=0.279\,{\rm GeV} \\
        Z_{0}=1.84 & B^{1/4}=0.145\,{\rm GeV} & \alpha_{s}=0.55
    \end{Bmatrix}
\end{equation}

Some people would like to refine these conclusions to reproduce spectra of hadrons with charm and bottom quarks 
in Refs.~\cite{Haxton:1980mc,Aerts:1980rf,Fleck:1989mb,He:2004px,Izatt:1981pt}, or glueball in Ref.~\cite{Carlson:1982er}.
Our considerations are: the parameters of MIT bag model are hard to be determined because tuning them always fails to cover all the confirmed states;
parameters describing physical properties in the same model shouldn't vary a lot.
Thus we will keep Eq.~(\ref{originalparas}) with little change in our work to calculate all the ground states, as this model is relatively general.

As noted above, free parameters are hard to reproduce all the states.
$B$ and $Z_{0}$ that largely lift up or lower down the whole spectra cannot reduce all the errors simultaneously, 
while light baryons with strange flavor and $K^{\ast}$ in Ref.~\cite{Johnson:1975zp} will not get close to zero-error together by changing $m_{s}$.
And if $\alpha_{s}$ is fixed to be $0.55$, the errors of heavy mesons will be up to $100\,{\rm MeV}$.
The only thing we believe that provides flexible variation is a running $\alpha_{s}$.

Inspired by QCD renormalization, $\alpha_{s}$ takes the semiempirical form in MIT bag model as 
\begin{equation}\label{alphaS}
    \alpha_{s}(R)=\frac{2\pi n}{9} \frac{1}{ln\left[\gamma+{\left(R\Lambda_{QCD}\right)}^{-n}\right]}
\end{equation}
where exponent $n$ can be either 1 or 2, while 1 is prefered for suppressing the value of $\alpha_{s}$,
$\Lambda_{QCD}$ denotes QCD scale taking value from $0.2\,{\rm GeV}$ to $0.5\,{\rm GeV}$,
and $\gamma$ is just a phenomenological variate meaning to avoid divergence.
Several expressions have been used in different works \cite{Haxton:1980mc,Aerts:1980rf,Carlson:1982er,He:2004px,Fleck:1989mb,Izatt:1981pt}, most of which only change $\gamma$, $\Lambda_{QCD}$ and $n$.
But there is a new form
\begin{equation}\label{alphaS-2004}
    \alpha_{s}(R)=\frac{0.470}{ln\left[1+{\left(0.420R\right)}^{-1}\right]}
\end{equation}
according to Da-Heng He's work \cite{He:2004px}, where $0.470$ is a coefficient, and $\Lambda_{QCD}=0.420\,{\rm GeV}$.

The problem emerges if we put Eq.~(\ref{alphaS-2004}) into Eq.~(\ref{Cij}), the errors of light hadrons will be enlarged significantly, since $\alpha_{s}=1.21$ when $R=5\,{\rm GeV}^{-1}$.
It's too large comparing with Eq.~(\ref{originalparas}), and strong coupling constant is actually smaller in hadrons when energy scale $\mu=1\,{\rm GeV}$.
For physical concerning, $\mu=1\,{\rm GeV}$ will fit $\alpha_{s}=0.55$ by zero-order approximation of QCD, getting $\Lambda_{QCD}=0.281\,{\rm GeV}$. 
Then we set $R=5\,{\rm GeV}^{-1}$ for aligning proton in MIT bag model, and fit 0.55 again by the form like Eq.~(\ref{alphaS-2004}) to come up with our conclusion
\begin{equation}\label{alphaS-mine}
    \alpha_{s}(R)=\frac{0.296}{ln\left[1+{\left(0.281R\right)}^{-1}\right]}
\end{equation}

In Fig.~\ref{fig:alphaS}, there are four running behaviors of $\alpha_{s}$,
in which only solid line provides significant variation with lower value at $R=5\,{\rm GeV}^{-1}$.
We use Eq.~(\ref{alphaS-mine}) to evaluate spectra of light hadrons listed in Table \ref{tab:lighthadron} with original results along side.
Although the errors seem to be comparatively greater while bag radius of proton is no longer $5\,{\rm GeV}^{-1}$,
a running $\alpha_{s}$ can remarkably reduce the errors of heavy hadrons, especially heavy mesons according to our calculation.

We now determine the masses of $c$, $b$ quarks and five kinds of binding energy.
In this work, doubly heavy mesons are used for obtaining binding energy, from which we are unable to fit $m_{c}$ and $m_{b}$.
An alternative approach chooses heavy-light mesons like $D$, $D^{\ast}$, $B$, $B^{\ast}$. 
Additionally, we find the meson states with $J^{P}=1^{-}$ being easier to lie on zero-error level, while singly heavy baryons show stable errors.
It can be checked out in Table \ref{tab:lightmeson}, \ref{tab:lightbaryon} and \ref{tab:1heavybaryon}.
We thus use $D^{\ast}$ and $B^{\ast}$ states to give
\begin{equation}\label{mcmb}
    \begin{Bmatrix}
        m_{c}=1.641\,{\rm GeV} & m_{b}=5.093\,{\rm GeV}
    \end{Bmatrix}
\end{equation}
Having masses of $c$, $b$ quarks, we then determine binding energy by $D_{s}^{\ast}$, $B_{s}^{\ast}$, $J/\psi$, $\Upsilon$ and $B_{c}^{\ast}$ giving
\begin{equation}\label{bingdings}
    \begin{Bmatrix}
        B_{cs}=-0.025\,{\rm GeV} & B_{cc}=-0.077\,{\rm GeV} \\ 
        B_{bs}=-0.032\,{\rm GeV} & B_{bb}=-0.128\,{\rm GeV} \\ 
        B_{bc}=-0.101\,{\rm GeV}
    \end{Bmatrix}
\end{equation}
where the mass $6.332\,{\rm GeV}$ of $B_{c}^{\ast}$ quotes Ref.~\cite{Ebert:2002pp}.

In this work, the binding energy $B_{QQ^{\prime}}$ takes value for color $\boldsymbol{\bar{3}}$, which scales by Eq.~(\ref{colorfc}) through color configurations.
For color $\boldsymbol{1}$, we have $2B_{QQ^{\prime}}$. For color $\boldsymbol{6}$, we have $-B_{QQ^{\prime}}/2$.
Moreover, binding energies between quark and antiquark within tetraquark are $5B_{QQ^{\prime}}/4$ and $B_{QQ^{\prime}}/2$ for $\boldsymbol{6}\otimes\boldsymbol{\bar{6}}$ and $\boldsymbol{\bar{3}}\otimes\boldsymbol{3}$ respectively, 
whose scale can be found in Eq.~(\ref{cfcT}).

We also calculate doubly and triply heavy baryons presented in Table \ref{tab:23heavybaryon}.
There are short summaries:

1. Some theoretical masses $M_{bag}$ depart from the experimental values about $30\,{\rm GeV}\sim 40\,{\rm GeV}$. 
Although they are not small errors, we still accept them up to the level following MIT bag model, in Table \ref{tab:lighthadron}, saying around $40\,{\rm GeV}$.

2. Table \ref{tab:lightmeson} and \ref{tab:heavymeson} list mesons without their antiparticles because they share the same mass, charge radius but minus magnetic moment.
Antiparticles can be neglected in studying tetraquarks as well.

3. Our results of masses for $\Xi_{cc}$ and $\Xi_{cc}^{\ast}$ in Table \ref{tab:23heavybaryon} are $3.604\,{\rm GeV}$ and $3.714\,{\rm GeV}$ respectively.
We note that the errors of excited states are usually little in strange and charmed baryons in this work. 
Considering that D.~Ebert has given $M\left(\Xi_{cc}^{\ast}\right)=3.727\,{\rm GeV}$ in Ref.~\cite{Ebert:2004ck}, we believe $3.714\,{\rm GeV}$ will be a close prediction.

4. Generally, MIT bag model discusses the magnetic moment ratio of hadron to proton, which is more precise than magnetic moment itself.
In Table \ref{tab:lightbaryon}, the predicted ratios are in good agreement with experimental data, for which we could further calculate heavy baryons and tetraquarks well.

5. The charge radius of proton is predicted to be $0.75\,{\rm fm}$ comparing with experimental value $0.84\,{\rm fm}$.
Our predictions of charge radii are smaller, so did MIT bag model \cite{Johnson:1975zp,DeGrand:1975cf}.

6. We calculate the confirmed states not for restudying mesons and baryons, but to prove that MIT bag model can determine spectrum well, from which we can further study multi-heavy baryons and tetraquarks.

Recall chromomagnetic mixing, that a diquark or antiquark carring different flavors beyond SU(2) isospin symmetry, can be symmetric or asymmetric in terms of flavor wave function.
At this point, we would study the mixing systems $(\phi^{B}\chi_{2}^{B},\phi^{B}\chi_{3}^{B})$ for baryons with $J^{P}={\frac{1}{2}}^{+}$, 
where $\Xi_{c}$ , $\Xi_{b}$, $\Xi_{bc}$, $\Omega_{bc}$ are involved.

We present the matrix of CMI for this mixing system in Appendix C, where the subscripts of $C_{ij}$ depend on flavor configurations. 
In this paper, we propose $(ns)c$ for $\Xi_{c}$, $(ns)b$ for $\Xi_{b}$, $(bc)n$ for $\Xi_{bc}$ and $(bc)s$ for $\Omega_{bc}$, since two light or heavy quarks are regarded to be easier to form diquark.
We also consider binding energies $B_{cs}$, $B_{bs}$, $B_{bc}$ and will add them to mass formula directly.

As noted in Sec.~\ref{Method}, variational mothed requires the mass formula with analytical eigenvalues of CMI matrix.
One will not put a set of initially guessed $x_{i}$, $R$ into matrix (\ref{mixing23}) and perform numerical variation, 
because even a mixing hadronic state has its unique dynamical information.
Following this principle, we diagonalize the matrix (\ref{mixing23}) getting two eigenvalues to construct mass formula.
Once $x_{i}$, $R$ are determined, eigenvectors can be evaluated for identfying and describing mixing states.

Table \ref{tab:mixingbaryon} reveals our results of mixing baryons.
In the second column, we notice that the mixing effects are not significant, even neglectable for singly heavy baryons.
Therefore the masses of mixing states can be compared with experimental data of $\Xi_{c}$, $\Xi_{c}^{\prime}$, $\Xi_{b}$ and $\Xi_{b}^{\prime}$ as listed in the fifth column, whose errors are acceptable in MIT bag model.
On the other hand, mixing effects will weaken as charm quarks are replaced by bottom quarks in $(ns)c$, or strange quarks are replaced by $n$ quarks in $(bc)s$, according to our results.
We believe this is due to breaking of Heavy Quark Symmetry and small contribution from off-diagonal elements of matrix (\ref{mixing23}).


\subsection{Singly Heavy Tetraquarks}
We now move on to singly heavy tetraquarks $nn\bar{s}\bar{c}$ and $nn\bar{s}\bar{b}$, while the states with three $n$ quarks will be neglected for stability.
To study chromomagnetic mixing of them, we introduce the mixing systems such as
$(\phi_{2}^{T}\chi_{3}^{T},\phi_{1}^{T}\chi_{6}^{T})$ with $J^{P}=0^{+}$ and $I=1$,
$(\phi_{1}^{T}\chi_{3}^{T},\phi_{2}^{T}\chi_{6}^{T})$ with $J^{P}=0^{+}$ and $I=0$,
$(\phi_{2}^{T}\chi_{2}^{T},\phi_{2}^{T}\chi_{4}^{T},\phi_{1}^{T}\chi_{5}^{T})$ with $J^{P}=1^{+}$ and $I=1$,
$(\phi_{1}^{T}\chi_{2}^{T},\phi_{1}^{T}\chi_{4}^{T},\phi_{2}^{T}\chi_{5}^{T})$ with $J^{P}=1^{+}$ and $I=0$.
Additionally, binding energies become diagonal matrix in mass formula, since the mixing systems contain two color configurations and spin states are orthorhombic.
One should diagonalize the sum of interaction matrices in calculating hadron mass.

Following variational principle, we will diagonalize second order matrix getting two analytical eigenvalues to construct mass formula as usual.
On the contrary, however, we are unable to perform the same procedure on three order matrix, whose eigenvalues are roots of a cubic equation.
At this point, we would rather scan three sets of $x_{i}$, $R$ for solving equation numerically to obtain minimal masses with three eigenvalues respectively.

Our results are shown in Table \ref{tab:1heavytetraquark}, where we focus on isosinglet $nn\bar{s}\bar{c}$ with $J^{P}=0^{+}$, whose masses are $2.934\,{\rm GeV}$ and $2.513\,{\rm GeV}$ for two mixing states respectively.
Considering that the experimental data was reported to be $2866\pm 7\,{\rm MeV}$ \cite{LHCb:2020kd} and Karliner has suggested $2863.4\pm 12\,{\rm MeV}$ via string-junction picture \cite{Karliner:2020vsi},
our prediction $2.934\,{\rm GeV}$ is larger even subtracted by model error $40\,{\rm MeV}$.
But if we rather calculate $\phi_{1}^{T}\chi_{3}^{T}$, $\phi_{2}^{T}\chi_{6}^{T}$ states neglecting mixing effects, as Karliner did for color $\boldsymbol{\bar{3}}\otimes\boldsymbol{3}$ configuration, the results are around $2.7\,{\rm GeV}$ and non-physical.
On the other hand, the masses among chromomagnetic mixing with $J^{P}=0^{+}$ has large splitting in this work, as well as in S.~Q.~Luo's one \cite{Luo:2017eub}, where the same CMI model is adopted.
Thus we hold on our prediction and mixing picture. The open-heavy-flavor exotic $X_{0}(2900)$ isn't likely to be isosinglet $nn\bar{s}\bar{c}$ tetraquark, unless a larger error $70\,{\rm MeV}$ for $K$ meson in Table \ref{tab:lightmeson} were accepted.


\subsection{Doubly Heavy Tetraquarks}
Finally, we study doubly heavy tetraquarks with strange number $S=0$, $S=1$ and $S=2$.
There are mixing systems such as $(\phi_{2}^{T}\chi_{3}^{T},\phi_{1}^{T}\chi_{6}^{T})$ with $J^{P}=0^{+}$ for isotriplet $nn\bar{Q}\bar{Q}$, $ns\bar{Q}\bar{Q}$ and $ss\bar{Q}\bar{Q}$,
$(\phi_{2}^{T}\chi_{5}^{T},\phi_{1}^{T}\chi_{4}^{T})$ with $J^{P}=1^{+}$ for isosinglet $nn\bar{Q}\bar{Q}$,
and $(\phi_{2}^{T}\chi_{2}^{T},\phi_{2}^{T}\chi_{5}^{T},\phi_{1}^{T}\chi_{4}^{T})$ with $J^{P}=1^{+}$ for $ns\bar{Q}\bar{Q}$.
$nn\bar{c}\bar{b}$ follows the same case of $nn\bar{s}\bar{c}$. We will neglect $ns\bar{c}\bar{b}$ for the mixing among six spin-color states, whose sextic equation cannot be solved directly.

We note that threshold study is not supported in this work for the uncertainty of hadron mass about $40\,{\rm MeV}$. 
Generally, the theoretical value should be examined with experimental data for accuracy, but if there is no confirmation of tetraquark state, we can only make predictions with error analysis.
Thus we propose $40\,{\rm MeV}$ from mesons and baryons, and proceed to tetraquarks roughly, which is not assuredly correct to undergo threshold study. 
Otherwise, we refer two works about chromomagnetic mixing and doubly heavy tetraquarks \cite{Luo:2017eub,Lu:2020rog}, where totally different conclusions about stability of isosinglet $nn\bar{b}\bar{b}$ with $J^{P}=1^{+}$ were suggested.
At this point, the discussions of model itself will be more significant than threshold.

Our results are listed in Table \ref{tab:2heavytetraquark1}, \ref{tab:2heavytetraquark0}, \ref{tab:2heavytetraquark1/2}, and summarized in Table \ref{tab:comparison} comparing with other's works.
Here we use CMI model to identify hadronic states, and perform MIT bag model to calculate dynamical properties. 
Ref.~\cite{Luo:2017eub} adopted CMI model and constituent quark model by absorbing dynamical effects into quark mass in scheme 1, or by introducing reference systems in scheme 2.
Ref.~\cite{Lu:2020rog} also applied CMI model but used relativized quark model to determine spectra.
Ref.~\cite{Ebert:2007rn} contains results of D.~Ebert's.

We find explicitly that our predictions are smaller than scheme 1 and larger than scheme 2 in Ref.~\cite{Luo:2017eub}. 
Recalling that Eq.~(\ref{MBm}) evaluates the contribution of each part of energy, scheme 1 will be a better analogy which just adds quark masses and CMI together.
The differences are: MIT bag model would rather calculate $C_{ij}$ then fitting them; this model distinguishs dynamical properties for any hadronic states by variational method.
Our calculations are more flexible than fixing all the parameters thus give lower values for stability.
Another difference we believe is the consideration of binding energy. Since CMI model is regarded to contain chromoelectric interaction \cite{Liu:2019zoy}, the short-distance Coulomb potential will be neglected automatically.
But it's important in this work which can reduce errors and suppress mass spectra for doubly heavy systems.

As addressed in the previous section, our results imply large mass splitting among chromomagnetic mixing. For singly heavy tetraquarks, we have splitting about $400\,{\rm MeV}$.
For doubly heavy sectors, we have $300\,{\rm MeV}$, while Ref.~\cite{Luo:2017eub} suggests $200\,{\rm MeV}$ for scheme 1.
Our wide splittings are due to variational method, where eigenvalues of CMI matrix don't share the same set of $x_{i}$, $R$.
Moreover, the mixing effects are also found to be less significant than S.~Q.~Luo's work for this reason.

There is a notable phenomenon comparing our predictions with Ref.~\cite{Lu:2020rog}, that the distinctions are regular for charmed or bottom sectors.
In $nn\bar{c}\bar{c}$ and $ns\bar{c}\bar{c}$ systems, our spectra are $150\,{\rm MeV}$ smaller.
In $nn\bar{b}\bar{b}$ and $ns\bar{b}\bar{b}$ systems, our spectra are $75\,{\rm MeV}$ larger.
In $ss\bar{c}\bar{c}$ systems, our spectra are $100\,{\rm MeV}$ smaller.
In $ss\bar{b}\bar{b}$ systems, our spectra are $100\,{\rm MeV}$ larger.
These behaviors remind us of errors in confirmed states, where bottom baryons are overestimated and charmed baryons are underestimated.
If relativized quark model is reliable for tetraquarks and Q.~F.~L\"u's work is precise enough, these distinctions may be our errors.
However, we cannot determine which one is closer to experimental data. The only subject available is error analysis from mesons and baryons to tetraquarks.


\section{Summaries and Discussions}\label{Discussions}
In this work, we have estimated the mass spectra, magnetic moments and charge radii for all the ground-state mesons, baryons and some tetraquarks, 
in MIT bag model with CMI model which describes intrinsical properties of hadronic states.
A new running behavior of strong coupling constant is proposed thus suppresses errors for confirmed states.
Short-distance chromoelectric interactions namely binding energies are adopted to reproduce doubly heavy systems.

The parameters are universal in this model. Most of them, including running $\alpha_{s}$, are determined by light hadrons.
Binding energies from heavy quarkonia can apply to baryons and tetraquarks.
Considering that previous works of this model only discuss specific systems with their parameters, our advantage is ability to study the whole ground-state spectra in a unified description.
The calculations on mesons and baryons aim to prove it with a model error about $40\,{\rm MeV}$.

The terms of chromoelectric interactions are neglected in CMI model, as well as in MIT bag model for their little contributions.
But binding energies exist among strange and heavy quarks in our conclusions.
They can offset errors for heavy quarkonia and give close predictions of singly heavy baryons as $\Omega_{c}^{\ast}$ and doubly heavy sectors as $\Xi_{cc}$.
The physical pictures of them is one-gluon-exchange between two relatively heavy quarks which locate at the center of hadron.
Here we just extract them as free parameters.

As mentioned in Sec. \ref{Parameters}, the only quantity that can reduce all the errors significantly is running $\alpha_{s}$. 
We have proposed a new running behavior with good estimation. However, the physical picture has not been revealed.
First, a phenomenological parameter $R$ takes replacement of energy scale $\mu$ in Eq.~(\ref{alphaS-mine}), which should rely on quark mass or flavor.
Indeed, bag radius $R$ will be smaller when bottom quarks are included, that somehow simulates QCD principle but is not precise enough.
And second, Eq.~(\ref{alphaS-mine}) is an empirical formula without any derivation.
Although it has made success in our work, we should still treat it discreetly.

Other than universal parameters, the advantages of MIT bag model are in three aspects.
First, it provides confinement picture as a sphere bag, where any constituent with color-spin configurations can be contained, thus we are able to calculate tetraquark states.
Theoretically, this model can be applied to any hadronic states even glueball and quark star. 
Second, masses and dynamical parameters $x_{i}$ are evaluated by variational method, which implies a great power of prediction.
And third, thanks to mass formula, the contributions to hadron mass from quark, vacuum energies and interactions are discrete explicitly.
The clear physical picture helps enrich our understanding on the internal of hadron, and further study the sum rule of hadron mass.

Our results of spectra have been presented with detailed discussions about model and error analysis. 
We emphasize that the model error $40\,{\rm MeV}$ was found in original work of MIT bag model. 
After introducing running $\alpha_{s}$, it emerges in our data for light hadrons, subsequently for all the confirmed states. 
Without possible examinations, we promote this error to tetraquarks and conclude that $X_{0}(2900)$ isn't an isosinglet $nn\bar{s}\bar{c}$ state whose distinction is up to $70\,{\rm MeV}$.
Meanwhile, the comparison of our results with that of relativized quark model may imply a larger error $75\sim 150\,{\rm MeV}$ for tetraquarks, which is remained to be proved.
Anyway, we have made many valuable predictions of mass spectra, magnetic moments and charge radii, for doubly and triply heavy baryons, and most tetraquarks. 
Magnetic moments have not been discussed systematacially but reliable as addressed in Sec. \ref{Parameters}.
And if the mass spectra are predicted well, so will magnetic moments.

        -\frac{4\sqrt{2}}{3}\gamma  & -\frac{8}{3}\theta            & -2\sqrt{2}\beta \\
        -4\gamma                    & -2\sqrt{2}\beta               & -\frac{4}{3}\eta
    \end{pmatrix}
\end{equation}
for $(\phi_{2}^{T}\chi_{2}^{T},\phi_{2}^{T}\chi_{5}^{T},\phi_{1}^{T}\chi_{1}^{T})$ mixing,
\begin{equation}\label{112mixing245}
    \begin{pmatrix}
        -\frac{2}{3}(2\alpha+5\beta)    & \frac{10\sqrt{2}}{3}\delta    & 4\delta \\
        \frac{10\sqrt{2}}{3}\delta      & -\frac{4}{3}\eta              & -2\sqrt{2}\beta \\
        4\delta                         & -2\sqrt{2}\beta               & -\frac{8}{3}\theta
    \end{pmatrix}
\end{equation}
for $(\phi_{1}^{T}\chi_{2}^{T},\phi_{1}^{T}\chi_{4}^{T},\phi_{2}^{T}\chi_{5}^{T})$ mixing,
where $\alpha=C_{12}+C_{34}$, $\beta=C_{13}+C_{14}+C_{23}+C_{24}$, $\gamma=C_{13}+C_{14}-C_{23}-C_{24}$, $\delta=C_{13}-C_{14}+C_{23}-C_{24}$, 
$\eta=C_{12}-3C_{34}$ and $\theta=3C_{12}-C_{34}$, refering Ref.~\cite{Luo:2017eub}.

And we also present binding energies
\begin{equation}
    B_{12}+B_{13}+B_{23}
\end{equation}
for $\phi^{B}$,
\begin{equation}
    -\frac{1}{2}B_{12}+\frac{5}{4}B_{13}+\frac{5}{4}B_{14}+\frac{5}{4}B_{23}+\frac{5}{4}B_{24}-\frac{1}{2}B_{34}
\end{equation}
for $\phi_{1}^{T}$, and
\begin{equation}
    B_{12}+\frac{1}{2}B_{13}+\frac{1}{2}B_{14}+\frac{1}{2}B_{23}+\frac{1}{2}B_{24}+B_{34}
\end{equation}
for $\phi_{2}^{T}$ respectively, where $B_{ij}$ takes value from Eq.~(\ref{bingdings}) and equals 0 if there is no short-distance binding.
