\begin{abstract}
We compute masses and magnetic moments of ground-state heavy baryons and tetraquarks with one and two open heavy flavors in MIT bag model. 
In addition to the parameters of standard bag model, we use masses of heavy mesons with $J^{P}=1^{-}$ to extract the effective masses of charm and bottom quarks, and binding energies assumed to exist among heavy quarks ($c$ and $b$) and strange quarks in the literatures. 
The predictions of doubly charmed baryons are to be $M(\Xi_{cc})=3.604\,{\rm GeV}$ and $M(\Xi_{cc}^{\ast})=3.714\,{\rm GeV}$, as well as the strange isosinglet tetraquark $ud\bar{s}\bar{c}$ with $J^{P}=0^{+}$ and mass $M\left(ud\bar{s}\bar{c}\right)=2.934$ GeV. 
Notable flavor-dependence is seen in further predictions of the bag radius and charge radii for mixing systems treating chromomagnetic interaction as perturbation. 
Our numerical variation indicates that the calculated mass spectra are comparable to perturbative treatment, which enables us to study $ns\bar{c}\bar{b}$ tetraquarks.

\maketitle\section{Introduction}
In 2017, the LHCb Collaboration at CERN discovered the doubly charmed baryon $\Xi_{cc}^{++}$ with $J^{P}={1/2}^{+}$ and the measured mass $3621.40\pm 0.78$ MeV \cite{Aaij:2017ueg}. 
This observation provides important experimental information about the strength of the interaction between two heavy quarks, and makes it possible to resolve the question 
that whether the tetraquarks $QQ\bar{q}\bar{q}$ with two open heavy quarks $Q$ and two light antiquarks $\bar{q}$ are stable or unstable against decaying into two $Q\bar{q}$ mesons \cite{Karliner:2017qjm,Eichten:2017ffp,Luo:2017eub}. 
More recently, LHCb Collaboration reported the first exotic state $X_{0}(2900)$ with open heavy flavors and mass $2866\pm 7$ MeV \cite{LHCb:2020kd}. 
It has been interpreted as an isosinglet tetraquark $cs\bar{u}\bar{d}$ in Ref.~\cite{Karliner:2020vsi}. 
These foundings aroused great interest in doubly heavy baryons and tetraquarks.

Various approaches have been employed to explore doubly heavy baryons and tetraquarks in the literatures, including potential quark models and bag model \cite{Fleck:1989mb,Ebert:2004ck,Bernotas:2012nz,He:2004px,Karliner:2014gca,Liu:2018euh} for doubly heavy baryons. 
For the tetraquarks, see Refs. \cite{Ali:2017jda,Liu:2019zoy} in recent reviews. 
Information like mass spectrum and other properties of heavy hadrons can be important in identifying and/or finding them eventually. 
For instance, a $\Xi_{cc}$ candidate with a mass about $100$ MeV lighter than the quark model prediciton around $3620$ MeV \cite{Ebert:2004ck,Karliner:2014gca} was observed previously in 2002, 
by the SELEX Collaboration at Fermilab \cite{Moinester:2002uw}, which was confirmed inaccurate in 2017.

In this work, we employ MIT bag model to systematically study masses and magnetic moments of singly and doubly heavy baryons and tetraquarks with one and two open heavy flavors. 
By the way, the masses and other properties of the ground state light mesons, heavy mesons are also calculated. For possible chromomagnetic mixing in $\Xi_{c}$, $\Xi_{b}$, $\Xi_{bc}$, $\Omega_{bc}$ with $J^{P}={\frac{1}{2}}^{+}$, 
we compute via numerical variation the mass splittings of them, comparing to perturbative treatment of chromomagnetic interactions.

In the past few decades, bag model have been applied to address mass spectra and other properties of the doubly heavy baryons \cite{Fleck:1989mb,He:2004px, Bernotas:2012nz} and multiquark hadrons, including light multiquark baryons with five and seven nonstrange quarks \cite{Strottman:1979qu}. 
A large running strong coupling $\alpha_{s}$ was applied to evaluate the mass spectra of doubly heavy baryons \cite{He:2004px}. 
A Coulomb-like interaction between heavy quarks in a bag is derived in Refs.~\cite{Haxton:1980mc,Aerts:1980rf}.

It is well known that bag model \cite{DeGrand:1975cf}, simple though it is, embodies two primary features of quantum chromodynamics (QCD): asymptotic freedom at short distance and confinement at long distance. 
We find that the MIT bag model, when equiped with a smaller running strong coupling $\alpha_{s}$ and binding energies which are assumed to exist among heavy quarks ($c$ and $b$) and strange quarks in the literatures, 
can reconcile the computation of the heavy hadrons with the known results for the light hadron. 
The mass errors of our calculation reduce to about 40 MeV for heavy hadrons, from which we proceed to predict the masses and other properties of the tetraquarks with one and two open heavy quarks.

This paper will be organized as follows. In Sec.~\ref{Method}, we review some basic relations of MIT bag model, including chromomagnetic interaction (CMI) among the quarks in bag. 
In Sec.~\ref{Parameters}, a systematic numerical calculation is performed for the established light and heavy baryons, with the optimal set of parameters obtained and the results for the mass spectra and other properties reproduced.
In Sec.~\ref{Hadrons}, we present a detailed predictions for masses and other propeties for doubly heavy baryons and doubly (open) heavy tetraquarks. 
The paper ends with summary and conclusions in Sec.~\ref{Discussions}.


\section{Method for MIT bag model and CMI}\label{Method}
\subsection{Mass Formula}
Treating hadron as a sphere bag, MIT bag model provides an approach to estimate masses and other properties of hadrons in their ground states \cite{Johnson:1975zp,DeGrand:1975cf}, 
in which the chromomagnetic interaction is derived from the energy of a sphere-like gluon field interacting with quark fields in bag \cite{DeGrand:1975cf}. 
The mass formula of hadron in MIT bag model is,
\begin{equation}\label{MBm}
    M\left(R\right)=\sum_{i=n,s,c,b} n_{i}\omega_{i}+\frac{4}{3}\pi R^{3}B-\frac{Z_{0}}{R}+\Delta M,
\end{equation}
\begin{equation}\label{freq}
    \omega_{i}=\left(m_{i}^{2}+\frac{x_{i}^{2}}{R^2}\right)^{1/2},
\end{equation}
where the first term is the kinematic energy of all quarks, the second is volume energy, the third is the zero-point-energy (ZPE) and $\Delta M$ is interaction energy among quarks in bag, on which we will address in this work. 
Here in Eq. (\ref{MBm}), $n_{i}$ is number of quark or antiquark in bag with mass $m_{i}$ and flavor $i$, where $i$ can be $n=u,d$, $s$, $c$ and $b$, $x_{i}=x_{i}(mR)$ is dimensionless dynamical parameters, 
$R$ is the bag radius to be determined variationally, $B$ is the bag constant and $Z_{0}$ is ZPE coefficient. $x_{i}$ and $R$ are related by an eigen equation
\begin{equation}\label{transc}
    \tan x_{i}=\frac{x_{i}}{1-m_{i}R-\left(m_{i}^{2}R^{2}+x_{i}^{2}\right)^{1/2}}.
\end{equation}

The interaction energy $\Delta M$ is composed of two energy terms:
(1) The chromoelectric interaction energy, due mainly to the short-ranged perturbative gluon exchange between quarks (antiquarks). 
Owing to its smallness for the relativistic light quarks involving the nonstrange quark $n$ ($u$ and $d$), this energy, scales mainly as $-\alpha_{s}/r_{ij}$, becomes sizable when both of two quarks $i$ and $j$ are massive and moving nonrelativistically. 
In present work, we treat this energy as sum of binding energies among heavy quarks and between heavy quark $Q$ and strange quark $s$ \cite{Karliner:2014gca,Karliner:2017elp,Karliner:2018bms}.
The net effect for this chromoelectric interaction amounts to introduction of five binding energies $B_{QQ^{\prime}}$ and $B_{Qs}$: $B_{cs}$, $B_{cc}$, $B_{bs}$, $B_{bb}$ and $B_{bc}$, 
which can be extracted from heavy mesons for the pair configuration $\boldsymbol{\bar{3}}_{c}$ and scaled to other color configurations.

(2) The chromomagnetic interaction energy, due to perturbative gluon exchange between quarks (antiquarks) $i$ and $j$,
\begin{equation}\label{CMI}
    H_{CMI}=-\sum_{i<j} \left(\boldsymbol{\lambda_{i}}\cdot \boldsymbol{\lambda_{j}}\right) \left(\boldsymbol{\sigma_{i}}\cdot \boldsymbol{\sigma_{j}}\right)C_{ij},
\end{equation}
where $\boldsymbol{\lambda}_{i}$ are Gell-Mann matrices, $\boldsymbol{\sigma}_{i}$ are the Pauli matrices, and $C_{ij}$ is the CMI parameter. This parameter is given, in MIT bag model, by
\begin{equation}\label{Cij}
    C_{ij}=3\frac{\alpha_{s}\left(R\right)}{R^{3}}\bar{\mu}_{i}\bar{\mu}_{j}I_{ij},
\end{equation}
\begin{equation}\label{muBari}
   \bar{\mu}_{i}=\frac{R}{6} \frac{4\alpha_{i}+2\lambda_{i}-3}{2\alpha_{i}\left(\alpha_{i}-1\right)+\lambda_{i}},
\end{equation}
\begin{equation}\label{Iij}
    I_{ij}=1+2\int_{0}^{R} \frac{{\rm d}r}{r^{4}} \bar{\mu}_{i}\bar{\mu}_{j}=1+F(x_{i}, x_{j}),
\end{equation}
with 
\begin{equation}\label{F}
    \begin{aligned}
        F(x_{i}, x_{j})={\left(x_{i} {\rm sin}^{2}x_{i}-\frac{3}{2}y_{i}\right)}^{-1} {\left(x_{j} {\rm sin^{2}}x_{j}-\frac{3}{2}y_{j}\right)}^{-1} \\ 
        \left\{-\frac{3}{2}y_{i}y_{j}-2x_{i}x_{j} {\rm sin}^{2}x_{i} {\rm sin}^{2}x_{j} +\frac{1}{2}x_{i}x_{j}\left[2x_{i} {\rm Si}(2x_{i}) \right.\right.\\ 
        \left.\left. +2x_{j} {\rm Si}(2x_{j}) -(x_{x}+x_{j}) {\rm Si}(2(x_{x}+x_{j})) \right.\right.\\ 
        \left.\left. -(x_{x}-x_{j}) {\rm Si}(2(x_{x}-x_{j})) \right] \right\}
    \end{aligned}
\end{equation}
where $\alpha_{i}\equiv\omega_{i}R$, $\lambda_{i}\equiv m_{i}R$, $\alpha_{s}\left(R\right)$ is running strong coupling constant, $\bar{\mu}_{i}$ is reduced magnetic moment without electric charge,
and $I_{ij}$ takes a rational function of $x_{i}$ and $x_{j}$ according to Ref.~\cite{DeGrand:1975cf}. 

In some applications of bag model \cite{Haxton:1980mc,Aerts:1980rf,Carlson:1982er,He:2004px,Fleck:1989mb}, the parameter $\alpha_{s}$ takes, inspired by QCD renormalization, a semiempirical form like
\begin{equation}\label{alphaS}
    \alpha_{s}(R)=\frac{2\pi n}{9} \frac{1}{ln\left[\gamma+{\left(R\Lambda_{QCD}\right)}^{-n}\right]},
\end{equation}
where exponent $n=1$ or 2, with 1 prefered for suppressing the value of $\alpha_{s}$, $\Lambda_{QCD}$ is the QCD scale ranging from $0.2$ GeV to $0.5$ GeV, and $\gamma$ is prefactor used to avoid divergence. 
Similar to Ref.~\cite{He:2004px}, we take $\Lambda_{QCD}=0.281$ GeV to set
\begin{equation}\label{alphaS-mine}
    \alpha_{s}(R)=\frac{0.296}{ln\left[1+{\left(0.281R\right)}^{-1}\right]},
\end{equation}
which is plotted in FIG.~\ref{fig:alphaS}. Among four running behaviors of $\alpha_{s}$ in the plot, the solid line provides notable variation and simultaneously gives a relative lower value of $\alpha_{s}$. 
In the case of light hadrons, mass spectra evaluated by Eq.~(\ref{alphaS-mine}) are listed in Table \ref{tab:lighthadron}, compared to that given by original MIT bag model.

Given the parameters, the quark mass $m_{i}$, bag constant $B$, the ZPE coefficient $Z_{0}$ and strong coupling constant $\alpha_{s}(R)$ depending on bag radius $R$, one can determine the respective bag radius $R$ for each hadron via variational method.
Thereby, $x_{i}$ and $R$ are sufficient to compute the ground-state masses and other static properties, like magnetic moments, the charge radius, from mesons to tetraquarks. 

We emphasize that, there is a unique set of $x_{i}$ and $R$ for a given hadonic state with known flavor-spin-color structure and certain CMI, for it  carries the unique dynamical information. 
This eventually hinders us to find an simple formally-analytic mass formula in the case of the chromomagnetic-mixing systems, since one has to perform variation analysis after diagonalizing CMI matrices. 
Meanwhile, a first-order perturbative computation is done for them for comparison treating CMI as a pertubation. 


\subsection{Chromomagnetic Structure}
To evaluate the mass splitting due to the chromomagnetic interaction in Eq.~(\ref{CMI}), in which $\boldsymbol{\lambda_{i}}$ should be replaced by $-\boldsymbol{\lambda_{i}^{\ast}}$, 
the CMI matrix has to be computed for a given hadron with certain $J^{P}$ quantum number so that its evaluation gives the respective mass splittings \cite{Luo:2017eub}. 
Here, we list all the flavor-spin-color wave functions for meson, baryon and tetraquark systems considered in this work, and present relevant formulas for the color and spin factors.

A ground-state meson with color wave function
\begin{equation}\label{colorM}
    \phi^{M}=\left| q_{1}\bar{q}_{2} \right\rangle,
\end{equation}
can have two spin states (one of vector like, and other of scalar like):
\begin{equation}\label{spinM}
    \chi_{1}^{M}={\left| q_{1}\bar{q}_{2} \right\rangle}_{1}, \quad \chi_{2}^{M}={\left| q_{1}\bar{q}_{2} \right\rangle}_{0},
\end{equation}
where subscript $J$ outside the bracket denotes the spin of hadron. The spin-color wave functions of a meson with spin $J=0,1$ are
\begin{equation}\label{colorspinM}
    \phi^{M}\chi_{1}^{M}={\left| q_{1}\bar{q}_{2} \right\rangle}_{1}, \quad \phi^{M}\chi_{2}^{M}={\left| q_{1}\bar{q}_{2} \right\rangle}_{0}.
\end{equation}

A baryon with color wave functions
\begin{equation}\label{colorB}
    \phi^{B}=\left| {\left(q_{1}q_{2}\right)}^{\bar{3}} q_{3} \right\rangle,
\end{equation}
can have three spin states
\begin{equation}\label{spinB}
    \begin{aligned}
        \chi_{1}^{B}={\left| {\left(q_{1}q_{2}\right)}_{1} q_{3} \right\rangle}_{3/2}, \\
        \chi_{2}^{B}={\left| {\left(q_{1}q_{2}\right)}_{1} q_{3} \right\rangle}_{1/2}, \\
        \chi_{3}^{B}={\left| {\left(q_{1}q_{2}\right)}_{0} q_{3} \right\rangle}_{1/2},
    \end{aligned}
\end{equation}
where $\left(q_{1}q_{2}\right)$ stands for a diquark in color $\boldsymbol{\bar{3}}_{c}$ with spin $J=1$ or $0$.

To write the flavor-spin-color wave function for a hadron, one has to consider the flavor symmetry. 
For a symmetric flavor wave function of $\left(q_{1}q_{2}\right)$, with isospin $I=1$ or identical flavors, we use a symbol $\delta_{12}^{S}=1$. 
For an asymmetric flavor wave function, with $I=0$, there will be a symbol $\delta_{12}^{A}=1$. 
If two quarks $q_{1}$ and $q_{2}$ carry different flavors beyond SU(2) isospin symmetry, there will be no specific symmetry information, and one can use $\delta_{12}^{S}=\delta_{12}^{A}=1$.
By Pauli principle, we get three flavor-spin-color wave functions for a baryon
\begin{equation}\label{colorspinB}
    \begin{aligned}
        \phi^{B}\chi_{1}^{B}={\left| {\left(q_{1}q_{2}\right)}_{1}^{\bar{3}} q_{3} \right\rangle}_{3/2} \delta_{12}^{S}, \\
        \phi^{B}\chi_{2}^{B}={\left| {\left(q_{1}q_{2}\right)}_{1}^{\bar{3}} q_{3} \right\rangle}_{1/2} \delta_{12}^{S}, \\
        \phi^{B}\chi_{3}^{B}={\left| {\left(q_{1}q_{2}\right)}_{0}^{\bar{3}} q_{3} \right\rangle}_{1/2} \delta_{12}^{A}.
    \end{aligned}
\end{equation}

In this position, a possible state mixing can enter due to chromomagnetic interaction. 
For example, $\Xi_{bc}$ and $\Xi_{bc}^{\prime}$ with flavor structure $(bc)s$ choosing $\delta_{12}^{S}=\delta_{12}^{A}=1$ can not distinguish $\phi^{B}\chi_{2}^{B}$ and $\phi^{B}\chi_{3}^{B}$ states by their $J^{P}$ quantum number, 
thus the physical state must be a mixing of them. See more details about colormagnetic mixing in Sec.~\ref{Parameters}.

A tetraquark with color structure $\boldsymbol{6}\otimes\boldsymbol{\bar{6}}$ or $\boldsymbol{\bar{3}}\otimes\boldsymbol{3}$ 
\begin{equation}\label{colorT}
    \phi_{1}^{T}=\left| {\left(q_{1}q_{2}\right)}^{6} {\left(\bar{q}_{3}\bar{q}_{4}\right)}^{\bar{6}} \right\rangle, \quad
    \phi_{2}^{T}=\left| {\left(q_{1}q_{2}\right)}^{\bar{3}} {\left(\bar{q}_{3}\bar{q}_{4}\right)}^{3} \right\rangle,
\end{equation}
can have six spin states
\begin{equation}\label{spinT}
    \begin{aligned}
        \chi_{1}^{T}={\left| {\left(q_{1}q_{2}\right)}_{1} {\left(\bar{q}_{3}\bar{q}_{4}\right)}_{1} \right\rangle}_{2}, \quad
        \chi_{2}^{T}={\left| {\left(q_{1}q_{2}\right)}_{1} {\left(\bar{q}_{3}\bar{q}_{4}\right)}_{1} \right\rangle}_{1}, \\
        \chi_{3}^{T}={\left| {\left(q_{1}q_{2}\right)}_{1} {\left(\bar{q}_{3}\bar{q}_{4}\right)}_{1} \right\rangle}_{0}, \quad
        \chi_{4}^{T}={\left| {\left(q_{1}q_{2}\right)}_{1} {\left(\bar{q}_{3}\bar{q}_{4}\right)}_{0} \right\rangle}_{1}, \\
        \chi_{5}^{T}={\left| {\left(q_{1}q_{2}\right)}_{0} {\left(\bar{q}_{3}\bar{q}_{4}\right)}_{1} \right\rangle}_{1}, \quad
        \chi_{6}^{T}={\left| {\left(q_{1}q_{2}\right)}_{0} {\left(\bar{q}_{3}\bar{q}_{4}\right)}_{0} \right\rangle}_{0},
    \end{aligned}
\end{equation}
which gives twelve base wave functions
\begin{equation}\label{colorspinT}
    \begin{aligned}
        \phi_{1}^{T}\chi_{1}^{T}={\left| {\left(q_{1}q_{2}\right)}_{1}^{6} {\left(\bar{q}_{3}\bar{q}_{4}\right)}_{1}^{\bar{6}} \right\rangle}_{2} \delta_{12}^{A}\delta_{34}^{A}, \\
        \phi_{2}^{T}\chi_{1}^{T}={\left| {\left(q_{1}q_{2}\right)}_{1}^{\bar{3}} {\left(\bar{q}_{3}\bar{q}_{4}\right)}_{1}^{3} \right\rangle}_{2} \delta_{12}^{S}\delta_{34}^{S}, \\
        \phi_{1}^{T}\chi_{2}^{T}={\left| {\left(q_{1}q_{2}\right)}_{1}^{6} {\left(\bar{q}_{3}\bar{q}_{4}\right)}_{1}^{\bar{6}} \right\rangle}_{1} \delta_{12}^{A}\delta_{34}^{A}, \\
        \phi_{2}^{T}\chi_{2}^{T}={\left| {\left(q_{1}q_{2}\right)}_{1}^{\bar{3}} {\left(\bar{q}_{3}\bar{q}_{4}\right)}_{1}^{3} \right\rangle}_{1} \delta_{12}^{S}\delta_{34}^{S}, \\
        \phi_{1}^{T}\chi_{3}^{T}={\left| {\left(q_{1}q_{2}\right)}_{1}^{6} {\left(\bar{q}_{3}\bar{q}_{4}\right)}_{1}^{\bar{6}} \right\rangle}_{0} \delta_{12}^{A}\delta_{34}^{A}, \\
        \phi_{2}^{T}\chi_{3}^{T}={\left| {\left(q_{1}q_{2}\right)}_{1}^{\bar{3}} {\left(\bar{q}_{3}\bar{q}_{4}\right)}_{1}^{3} \right\rangle}_{0} \delta_{12}^{S}\delta_{34}^{S}, \\
        \phi_{1}^{T}\chi_{4}^{T}={\left| {\left(q_{1}q_{2}\right)}_{1}^{6} {\left(\bar{q}_{3}\bar{q}_{4}\right)}_{0}^{\bar{6}} \right\rangle}_{1} \delta_{12}^{A}\delta_{34}^{S}, \\
        \phi_{2}^{T}\chi_{4}^{T}={\left| {\left(q_{1}q_{2}\right)}_{1}^{\bar{3}} {\left(\bar{q}_{3}\bar{q}_{4}\right)}_{0}^{3} \right\rangle}_{1} \delta_{12}^{S}\delta_{34}^{A}, \\
        \phi_{1}^{T}\chi_{5}^{T}={\left| {\left(q_{1}q_{2}\right)}_{0}^{6} {\left(\bar{q}_{3}\bar{q}_{4}\right)}_{1}^{\bar{6}} \right\rangle}_{1} \delta_{12}^{S}\delta_{34}^{A}, \\
        \phi_{2}^{T}\chi_{5}^{T}={\left| {\left(q_{1}q_{2}\right)}_{0}^{\bar{3}} {\left(\bar{q}_{3}\bar{q}_{4}\right)}_{1}^{3} \right\rangle}_{1} \delta_{12}^{A}\delta_{34}^{S}, \\
        \phi_{1}^{T}\chi_{6}^{T}={\left| {\left(q_{1}q_{2}\right)}_{0}^{6} {\left(\bar{q}_{3}\bar{q}_{4}\right)}_{0}^{\bar{6}} \right\rangle}_{0} \delta_{12}^{S}\delta_{34}^{S}, \\
        \phi_{2}^{T}\chi_{6}^{T}={\left| {\left(q_{1}q_{2}\right)}_{0}^{\bar{3}} {\left(\bar{q}_{3}\bar{q}_{4}\right)}_{0}^{3} \right\rangle}_{0} \delta_{12}^{A}\delta_{34}^{A}.
    \end{aligned}
\end{equation}

For all relevant color wave functions see Appendix A and for relevant spin wave functions see Appendix B. Thus, one can calculate the color and spin factors in Eq.~(\ref{CMI}) with the help of the following methods
\begin{equation}\label{colorfc}
    {\left\langle \boldsymbol{\lambda_{i}}\cdot \boldsymbol{\lambda_{j}} \right\rangle}_{nm}=
    \sum_{\alpha=1}^{8} Tr\left(c_{in}^{\dagger}\lambda^{\alpha}c_{im}\right) Tr\left(c_{jn}^{\dagger}\lambda^{\alpha}c_{jm}\right),
\end{equation}
\begin{equation}\label{spinfc}
    {\left\langle \boldsymbol{\sigma_{i}}\cdot \boldsymbol{\sigma_{j}} \right\rangle}_{xy}=
    \sum_{\alpha=1}^{3} Tr\left(\chi_{ix}^{\dagger}\sigma^{\alpha}\chi_{iy}\right) Tr\left(\chi_{jx}^{\dagger}\sigma^{\alpha}\chi_{jy}\right),
\end{equation}
where $n$, $m$ and $x$, $y$ indicate the specific color and spin states respectively, $i$, $j$ are the indexes of quarks or antiquarks, and the functions $c$ and $\chi$ are the respective basis vectors in the color and spin spaces.
A set of non-mixing hadronic states with their respective CMI's are listed in Table \ref{tab:CMI}. 

Now, we are in the position to construct the matrix formula of CMI energy (\ref{CMI}) and diagonalize it and thereby to minimize the obtained mass formula. 
After adding the bag energy and the binding energy (for heavy quark pair and for a heavy quark and a strange quark), we can determine the dynamical parameters $x_{i}$ and $R$ and thereby obtain the full bag wave function for a hadron.

\subsection{Hadronic Properties}
Knowing the parameters $x_{i}$ and $R$ describing a hadronic state, one can subsequently calculate the charge radius and magnetic moment. 
Following the standard method, one can firstly evaluate the contribution of a quark or an antiquark $i$ with electric charge $Q_{i}$ on charge radius \cite{DeGrand:1975cf}
\begin{equation}\label{rEi}
    \begin{aligned}
        {\left\langle r_{E}^{2} \right\rangle}_{i}&=
        Q_{i}R^{2} \frac{\alpha_{i} \left[2x_{i}^{2}\left(\alpha_{i}-1\right)+4\alpha_{i}+2\lambda_{i}-3\right]}
        {3x_{i}^{2} \left[2\alpha_{i}\left(\alpha_{i}-1\right)+\lambda_{i}\right]} \\
        &-Q_{i}R^{2} \frac{\lambda_{i} \left[4\alpha_{i}+2\lambda_{i}-2x_{i}^{2}-3\right]}
        {2x_{i}^{2} \left[2\alpha_{i}\left(\alpha_{i}-1\right)+\lambda_{i}\right]}.
    \end{aligned}
\end{equation}
Then, one can directly sum up Eq.~(\ref{rEi}) to give the charge radius of a hadronic state \cite{Chodos:1974pn}
\begin{equation}\label{rEsum}
    r_{E}={\left| \sum\nolimits_{i} {\left\langle r_{E}^{2} \right\rangle}_{i} \right|}^{1/2}.
\end{equation}
Note that Eq.~(\ref{rEsum}) also holds true for the chromomagnetic mixing systems having the identical quark constituents.

For evaluating magnetic moment, one can use the following equations \cite{DeGrand:1975cf,Wang:2016dzu}
\begin{equation}\label{mui}
    \mu_{i}=Q_{i}\bar{\mu}_{i}=Q_{i}\frac{R}{6} \frac{4\alpha_{i}+2\lambda_{i}-3}{2\alpha_{i}\left(\alpha_{i}-1\right)+\lambda_{i}},
\end{equation}
\begin{equation}\label{musum}
    \mu=\left\langle \psi_{spin} \left| \sum\nolimits_{i}g_{i}\mu_{i}S_{iz} \right| \psi_{spin} \right\rangle,
\end{equation}
where $S_{iz}$ is the third component of spin for an individual quark or antiquark, and $g_{i}=2$ is the Lander factor. 
If the chromomagnetic mixing enters, the total spin wave function becomes
\begin{equation}
    \left| \psi_{spin} \right\rangle=C_{1}\chi_{1}+C_{2}\chi_{2},
\end{equation}
and the magnetic moment becomes
\begin{equation}\label{mixingmu}
    \mu=C_{1}^{2}\mu\left(\chi_{1}\right)+C_{2}^{2}\mu\left(\chi_{2}\right)+2C_{1}C_{2}\mu^{\rm tr}\left(\chi_{1}, \chi_{2}\right),
\end{equation}
with $\mu^{\rm tr}$ the transition moment \cite{Bernotas:2012nz} and the set of $C_{1}$, $C_{2}$ the eigenvector for mixing state.
Given the spin wave functions in Appendix B, we derive the magnetic moments for all the spin states and the mixing systems involved in this work, and list them collectively in Table \ref{tab:musum}.

Note that the transition moments in mixing systems are not symmetric under the exchange of the quarks $q_{1}$ and $q_{2}$ or $q_{3}$ and $q_{4}$ in the flavor wave functions, respectively. 
While isospin shall not be considered in constructing mass formula, it actually distinguishes the magnetic moments, between $\left(ud\right)s$ and $\left(du\right)s$ states for instance,
by the following wave functions with isospin involved
\begin{equation}
    {\left| 1,0\right\rangle}_{I}=\frac{1}{\sqrt{2}} \left(ud+du\right), \quad
    {\left| 0,0\right\rangle}_{I}=\frac{1}{\sqrt{2}} \left(ud-du\right).
\end{equation}
Physically, one has to employ the above two states to evaluate magnetic moment, but the transition moments vanish eventually. 
Therefore, there is no transition moment when the quark constituents follow SU(2) isospin symmetry, leaving only the terms with $C_{i}^{2}$ in Eq.~(\ref{mixingmu}).


\section{Determination of Parameters}\label{Parameters}
In MIT bag model, the parameters quark mass $m_{i}$, bag constant $B$, coefficient $Z_{0}$ of ZPE and strong coupling constant $\alpha_{s}$ are determined based on the mass spectra of the light hadrons $N$, $\Delta$, $\omega$ and $\Omega$ in their ground states. 
The results reads \cite{DeGrand:1975cf}
\begin{equation}\label{originalparas}
    \begin{Bmatrix}
        m_{n}=0\,{\rm GeV} & m_{s}=0.279\,{\rm GeV} \\
        Z_{0}=1.84 & B^{1/4}=0.145\,{\rm GeV} & \alpha_{s}=0.55
    \end{Bmatrix}.
\end{equation}

We assume the values of the parameters in Eq.~(\ref{originalparas}) to be reasonable for both of light and heavy hadrons, 
with only exception that the strong coupling $\alpha_{s}$ can change continuously with the size $R$ of hadron around $0.55$, as given by Eq. (\ref{alphaS-mine}). 
In fact, one can check easily that the fixed value of $\alpha_{s}$, near $0.55$ for instance, can lead to the mismatch error with respective to the measured masses of heavy mesons as large as $100$ MeV.

One remaining task is to determine the effective masses $m_{c}$ and $m_{b}$ of the charm and bottom quarks, as well as five kinds of binding energy $B_{QQ^{\prime}}$. 
In this work, we choose the heavy-light mesons $D^{\ast}$ and $B^{\ast}$ to extract the heavy quark masses $m_{c}$ and $m_{b}$. 
Usage of them gives rise to
\begin{equation}\label{mcmb}
    \begin{Bmatrix}
        m_{c}=1.641\,{\rm GeV}, & m_{b}=5.093\,{\rm GeV}
    \end{Bmatrix}.
\end{equation}
Substracting the heavy quark mass $m_{Q}$, one can extract the residual masse from the measured meson mass and predicted one, to find the binding energy. 
Usage of the mesons $D_{s}^{\ast}$, $B_{s}^{\ast}$, $J/\psi$, $\Upsilon$ and $B_{c}^{\ast}$ gives 
\begin{equation}\label{bingdings}
    \begin{Bmatrix}
        B_{cs}=-0.025\,{\rm GeV} & B_{cc}=-0.077\,{\rm GeV} \\
        B_{bs}=-0.032\,{\rm GeV} & B_{bb}=-0.128\,{\rm GeV} \\
        B_{bc}=-0.101\,{\rm GeV}
    \end{Bmatrix},
\end{equation}
for the color configuration $\boldsymbol{\bar{3}}$. 
Here in computation, we have used the mass $6.332$ GeV of $B_{c}^{\ast}$ predicted in Ref.~\cite{Ebert:2002pp}, due to the absence of measured mass for $B_{c}^{\ast}$.

In this work, the binding energy $B_{QQ^{\prime}}$ is determined for color $\boldsymbol{\bar{3}}$, which scaled, by Eq.~(\ref{colorfc}) to other color configurations. 
For color $\boldsymbol{1}$, we have $2B_{QQ^{\prime}}$. For color $\boldsymbol{6}$, we have $-B_{QQ^{\prime}}/2$ . 
In addition, the binding between quark and antiquark within tetraquark are $5B_{QQ^{\prime}}/4$ and $B_{QQ^{\prime}}/2$ for $\boldsymbol{6}\otimes\boldsymbol{\bar{6}}$ and $\boldsymbol{\bar{3}}\otimes\boldsymbol{3}$ respectively. 
These scalings can be obtained by color factors (\ref{cfcT}) and found in Eqs.~(\ref{ph1binding},\ref{ph2binding}).

In Tables \ref{tab:lightmeson}, \ref{tab:heavymeson}, \ref{tab:lightbaryon}, \ref{tab:1heavybaryon} and Table \ref{tab:23heavybaryon}, we list the computed masses, magnetic moments and charge radii for all established hadrons in their lowest-lying states without mixing. 
The results for the doubly and triply heavy baryons are also presented. Some remarks are in order: 

(1) Only the well-established hadrons are used in this work which are enough to fix the model parameters. 
Some of the computed masses $M_{bag}$ deviates from the measured masses at the level of errors about $30\sim 40$ GeV, within the error range of MIT bag model, as shown in Table \ref{tab:lighthadron}.

(2) The anti-particles of mesons are not listed in Table \ref{tab:lightmeson} and \ref{tab:heavymeson} because they share the same masses, charge radii but minus magnetic moments in comparison with the mesons in Tables. 
For the same reason, we have ignored the anti-particles of the tetraquarks considered as well.

(3) In Table \ref{tab:23heavybaryon}, our mass predictions $3.604$ GeV for the doubly charm baryon $\Xi_{cc}$ and $3.714$ GeV for $\Xi_{cc}^{\ast}$, 
are comparable to the quark model prediction $M\left(\Xi_{cc}^{\ast}\right)=3.727$ GeV \cite{Ebert:2004ck}, and also to $3706\pm 28\,{\rm MeV}$ and $3692\pm 28\,{\rm MeV}$ by the lattice QCD \cite{PACS-CS:2013vie,Brown:2014ena} respectively.

(4) In Table \ref{tab:lightbaryon}, the predicted magnetic moments are in good agreement with the measured values, from which we could further calculate the magnetic moments for heavy baryons and tetraquarks well.

(5) Our prediction $0.75\,{\rm fm}$ for the proton charge radius is lower than the measured value $0.84\,{\rm fm}$, similar to original MIT bag model \cite{Johnson:1975zp,DeGrand:1975cf}.

Recall a chromomagnetic mixing, in which a diquark or antiquark with different flavors, beyond SU(2) isospin symmetry, has no symmetry information in flavor space. 
To begin with, we first consider the mixing system $(\phi^{B}\chi_{2}^{B},\phi^{B}\chi_{3}^{B})$ for baryons with $J^{P}={\frac{1}{2}}^{+}$, where the baryons $\Xi_{c}$, $\Xi_{b}$, $\Xi_{bc}$ and $\Omega_{bc}$ are involved.

Given the CMI's calculated with the wave functions in color and spin space (Appendix A and B), one can construct (Appendix C) the CMI matrices in the chromomagnetic mixing system, where the subscripts of $C_{ij}$ denote the flavor configurations. 
We take the diquark-quark configurations $(ns)c$ for $\Xi_{c}$, $(ns)b$ for $\Xi_{b}$, $(bc)n$ for $\Xi_{bc}$ and $(bc)s$ for $\Omega_{bc}$. 

First of all, one has to diagonalize the matrix (\ref{mixing23}) to get two eigenvalues to find mass formulas. 
Once $x_{i}$, $R$ are determined by variational method, the eigenvectors of the matrix (\ref{mixing23}) can be evaluated numerically and thereby be used to identify and denote the mixing states.
In addition, we add the binding energies $B_{cs}$, $B_{bs}$ and $B_{bc}$ to the shifted mass $\Delta M$ directly. 

In Table \ref{tab:mixingbaryon}, we list our computed results of masses and other properties for the mixing systems of heavy baryons. 
The second columns of bag radius and predicted masse are the results of treating CMI as a perturbation, where we found the bag radii are only flavor-dependent and masses are close to that from variational method.
As shown in the second column of Table, the net effects of the state mixing are not so significant, even negligible in the case of singly heavy baryons. 
We believe this is due to higher SU(3) flavor and heavy quark symmetry contributing to the small off-diagonal elements in matrix (\ref{mixing23}). 
Thus, the masses of the mixing states are comparable with the measured masses of $\Xi_{c}$, $\Xi_{c}^{\prime}$, $\Xi_{b}$ and $\Xi_{b}^{\prime}$, as seen in the fifth column with reasonable errors.


\subsection{Singly Heavy Tetraquarks}
Let us consider singly heavy tetraquarks $nn\bar{s}\bar{c}$ and $nn\bar{s}\bar{b}$. To find chromomagnetic mixing of them, we introduce the mixing systems such as
$(\phi_{2}^{T}\chi_{3}^{T},\phi_{1}^{T}\chi_{6}^{T})$ with $J^{P}=0^{+}$ and $I=1$,
$(\phi_{1}^{T}\chi_{3}^{T},\phi_{2}^{T}\chi_{6}^{T})$ with $J^{P}=0^{+}$ and $I=0$,
$(\phi_{2}^{T}\chi_{2}^{T},\phi_{2}^{T}\chi_{4}^{T},\phi_{1}^{T}\chi_{5}^{T})$ with $J^{P}=1^{+}$ and $I=1$ and
$(\phi_{1}^{T}\chi_{2}^{T},\phi_{1}^{T}\chi_{4}^{T},\phi_{2}^{T}\chi_{5}^{T})$ with $J^{P}=1^{+}$ and $I=0$.
Further, the binding energy matrices become diagonal in mass formula since the mixing states have two color configurations while spin states are orthogonal. 
Diagonalizing should be applied to the sum of the interaction matrices when calculating the hadron mass.

Following variational principle, we diagonalize the 2-by-2 matrix to solve two analytical eigenvalues and then construct the mass formula as usual.
The application of the same procedure on the 3-by-3 matrix is, however, not straightforward, for which the eigenvalues are some roots of a cubic equation.
For this, we choose to numerically scan three sets of $x_{i}$ and $R$ to solve the cubic equation numerically so that one can obtain minimal masses with three root eigenvalues.

Our numerical results are shown in Table \ref{tab:1heavytetraquark}, with a notable tetraquark of a isosinglet $nn\bar{s}\bar{c}$ with $J^{P}=0^{+}$, having the masses $2.934$ GeV and $2.513$ GeV for two mixing states, respectively.
Considering that the measured mass of $X_{0}(2900)$ was reported to be $2866\pm 7$ MeV \cite{LHCb:2020kd} and the quark model prediction is about $2863.4\pm 12$ MeV\cite{Karliner:2020vsi}, our mass prediction $2.934$ GeV is larger even if the model error $40$ MeV is subtracted. 
Instead of this, if we rather ignore the mixing effects and calculate the masses of the $\phi_{1}^{T}\chi_{3}^{T}$ and $\phi_{2}^{T}\chi_{6}^{T}$ states directly, as Karliner suggested for the color $\boldsymbol{\bar{3}}\otimes\boldsymbol{3}$ configuration, the resulted masses are around $2.7$ GeV and non-physical. 

On the other hand, the masses due to chromomagnetic mixing with $J^{P}=0^{+}$ leads to a large splitting, as well as the prediction \cite{Luo:2017eub}, where the same CMI model is adopted. 
This suggests that the open-heavy-flavor exotic $X_{0}(2900)$ is not likely to be isosinglet $nn\bar{s}\bar{c}$ tetraquark, unless a larger error $70$ MeV for the $K$ meson in Table \ref{tab:lightmeson} were accepted.


\subsection{Doubly Heavy Tetraquarks}
Finally, let us consider the doubly heavy tetraquarks with strange number $S\leq 2$. Possible mixing can occur in the systems such as 
$(\phi_{2}^{T}\chi_{3}^{T},\phi_{1}^{T}\chi_{6}^{T})$ with $J^{P}=0^{+}$ for isotriplet $nn\bar{Q}\bar{Q}$, $ns\bar{Q}\bar{Q}$ and $ss\bar{Q}\bar{Q}$,
$(\phi_{2}^{T}\chi_{5}^{T},\phi_{1}^{T}\chi_{4}^{T})$ with $J^{P}=1^{+}$ for isosinglet $nn\bar{Q}\bar{Q}$, and 
$(\phi_{2}^{T}\chi_{2}^{T},\phi_{2}^{T}\chi_{5}^{T},\phi_{1}^{T}\chi_{4}^{T})$ with $J^{P}=1^{+}$ for $ns\bar{Q}\bar{Q}$. 
The case of the state $nn\bar{c}\bar{b}$ follows the same case of $nn\bar{s}\bar{c}$. 
We rather consider the mixing systems 
$(\phi_{2}^{T}\chi_{3}^{T},\phi_{2}^{T}\chi_{6}^{T},\phi_{1}^{T}\chi_{3}^{T},\phi_{1}^{T}\chi_{6}^{T})$ with $J^{P}=0^{+}$, 
$(\phi_{2}^{T}\chi_{2}^{T},\phi_{2}^{T}\chi_{4}^{T},\phi_{2}^{T}\chi_{5}^{T},\phi_{1}^{T}\chi_{2}^{T},\phi_{1}^{T}\chi_{4}^{T},\phi_{1}^{T}\chi_{5}^{T})$ with $J^{P}=1^{+}$, and
$(\phi_{2}^{T}\chi_{1}^{T},\phi_{1}^{T}\chi_{1}^{T})$ with $J^{P}=2^{+}$ for $ns\bar{c}\bar{b}$.
These states are only calculated by perturbative method since the sextic equations are required to be solved directly.

We note that given none of confirmed tetraquark state available, we can only make predictions with error analysis.
For the different conclusions about stability of isosinglet $nn\bar{b}\bar{b}$ with $J^{P}=1^{+}$, we refer to two calculations about chromomagnetic mixing for the doubly heavy tetraquarks \cite{Luo:2017eub,Lu:2020rog}. 
Thus the near-threshold effect is not accessible to address in this work owing to the uncertainty of hadron mass about $40$ MeV. 

We use CMI model to identify the doubly heavy tetraquarks, and use variational method to compute the dynamical properties for these exotic hadrons. 
Our computed results for the doubly heavy tetraquarks are listed in Tables \ref{tab:2heavytetraquark1}, \ref{tab:2heavytetraquark0} and \ref{tab:2heavytetraquark1/2}, with perturbative results attached. 
They are summarized in Table \ref{tab:comparison}, compared with other's works cited. In these works, Ref.~\cite{Luo:2017eub} has adopted the CMI model and Ref.~\cite{Lu:2020rog} used relativized quark model with CMI to compute the hadron spectra. 

As stated in the previous sections, our results imply larger mass splittings among the chromomagnetic mixing systems. For singly heavy tetraquarks, the mass splitting is about $400$ MeV. For the doubly heavy sectors, the splitting can be reduced to $300$ MeV. 
The reasons are twofold: 
(1) Compared to the CMI model, the MIT bag model calculates $C_{ij}$ and then compute hadron masses, giving different dynamical properties for hadronic states with the help of variational method. 
(2) Adding of binding energy among heavy quarks, stemming from chromoelectric interaction, being Coulomb-like interaction, reconciles the predictions for the light hadrons and the heavy sectors in this work. 

Comparing our predictions with Ref.~\cite{Lu:2020rog}, a notable phenomenon is that the mass distinctions between two calculations are of regular for charmed or bottom sectors. 
For instance, for the systems $nn\bar{c}\bar{c}$ and $ns\bar{c}\bar{c}$, our spectra are $150$ MeV smaller while they are $75$ MeV larger for the systems $nn\bar{b}\bar{b}$ and $ns\bar{b}\bar{b}$. 
In the systems of $ss\bar{c}\bar{c}$ , our mass predictions are $100$ MeV smaller but $100$ MeV larger for the systems $ss\bar{b}\bar{b}$ systems. 
These regular mass distinctions remind us of errors in established states, where bottom baryons are overestimated and charmed baryons are underestimated. 
Thus, it remains to make a systematic error analysis, staring from the light hadrons to heavy hadrons, and thereby giving reliable prediction for the heavy tetraquarks.


\section{Summary and Discussions}\label{Discussions}
In this work, we have estimated the mass spectra, magnetic moments and charge radii for all the ground-state mesons, baryons and some tetraquarks, in MIT bag model with CMI model which describes intrinsical properties of hadronic states. 
The model parameters are found to be universal for light hadrons and heavy hadrons, most of which, including running strong coupling, are determined from light hadrons.

The calculations on mesons and baryons aim to prove it with a model error about $40$ MeV. Differing from previous works of bag model, the strong coupling is running from $0.4$ to $0.6$ when bag radius varies in $R=3\sim 6$ GeV$^{-1}$.

The binding energies, assumed to exist among strange and heavy quarks, can offset errors for heavy quarkonia and give close predictions of singly heavy baryons as $\Omega_{c}^{\ast}$ and doubly heavy sectors as $\Xi_{cc}$. 
This added binding energy can be explained by one-gluon-exchange between two relatively heavy quarks which locate at the center of hadron, and can be made to be universal for the whole hadron spectrum. 

In princple, the bag model can be applied to any hadronic states, with CMI matrices calculable explicitly. This helps to enrich our understanding on the structures and mass composition of hadron. 
We promote the error of $40$ MeV to tetraquarks and conclude that $X_{0}(2900)$ isn't an isosinglet $nn\bar{s}\bar{c}$ state whose distinction is up to $70$ MeV.
